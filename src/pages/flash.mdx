<section id="intro">
  <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p>
    Every storage medium before this one had a motor. Punch cards were fed through
    rollers, drums spun, platters rotated, discs whirled under a laser. In 1991,
    SanDisk shipped the first commercial <strong>solid-state drive</strong> — a
    20 MB unit with no motor at all. Data was trapped as electrical charge inside
    silicon, and for the first time, "seeking" a piece of data meant nothing: every
    cell was equally close.
  </p>
  <p>
    It took almost two decades for flash to become cheap enough to replace the hard
    drive in everyday computers. But once <strong>NAND flash</strong> caught up in
    price, it changed the shape of the memory hierarchy — closing the long-standing
    gap between fast, volatile RAM and slow, mechanical secondary storage.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="nand">
  <h2>NAND Flash Memory Architecture</h2>
  <p>
    A NAND flash chip stores data in <strong>floating-gate transistors</strong> — a
    normal transistor with a second, electrically isolated gate buried inside it.
    Trapping electrons on that floating gate raises the cell's threshold voltage;
    removing them lowers it. Because the gate is insulated, the charge stays put
    with no power applied — this is what makes flash <strong>non-volatile</strong>.
  </p>

  <p>
    Cells are organized into <strong>pages</strong> (the smallest unit that can be
    written) and pages are grouped into <strong>blocks</strong> (the smallest unit
    that can be erased). A cell can only hold one voltage state at a time, so more
    bits per cell means finer voltage steps and a noisier read:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>SLC</strong> — 1 bit/cell, 2 voltage states, fastest and most durable.</li>
    <li><strong>MLC</strong> — 2 bits/cell, 4 voltage states.</li>
    <li><strong>TLC</strong> — 3 bits/cell, 8 voltage states, the mainstream consumer standard.</li>
    <li><strong>QLC</strong> — 4 bits/cell, 16 voltage states, cheapest per gigabyte, shortest lifespan.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    NAND has one quirk that shapes everything above it: it can write to an empty
    page, but it <strong>cannot overwrite</strong> a page directly — the whole block
    must be erased first. An SSD controller works around this by writing updates to
    a fresh page elsewhere and marking the old one invalid, then later copying the
    still-valid pages out of a block and erasing it wholesale. This background
    process is called <strong>garbage collection</strong>.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="lifespan">
  <h2>Wear Leveling and Flash Lifespan</h2>
  <p>
    Each erase cycle stresses the oxide layer around the floating gate a little
    more. Eventually a cell can no longer reliably hold a charge, so every NAND
    cell has a finite budget of <strong>program/erase (P/E) cycles</strong> —
    roughly 100,000 for SLC, down to a few hundred for QLC.
  </p>
  <p>
    If a drive always rewrote the same "hot" blocks — a swap file, a database log —
    those blocks would wear out in weeks while the rest of the chip sat untouched.
    <strong> Wear leveling</strong> algorithms prevent this by spreading writes
    evenly across the whole chip:
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Dynamic wear leveling</strong> — new writes are steered toward the least-worn free blocks.</li>
    <li><strong>Static wear leveling</strong> — even data that never changes is occasionally relocated, so its block re-enters the rotation instead of sitting idle forever.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Drives also reserve a slice of raw capacity that the user never sees, called
    <strong> over-provisioning</strong>, to give the controller room to juggle
    garbage collection and wear leveling without running out of free blocks. The
    <strong> TRIM</strong> command lets the operating system tell the drive which
    pages are no longer in use, so the controller can erase them ahead of time
    instead of scrambling during a write.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="interfaces">
  <h2>SATA vs. NVMe Interfaces</h2>
  <p>
    An SSD is only as fast as the road connecting it to the CPU. Early SSDs were
    built into the 2.5" form factor and spoke <strong>SATA</strong> — a protocol
    and connector designed in 2003 for spinning platters. SATA III tops out around
    6 Gbps (about 550 MB/s after overhead), and its command layer, <strong>AHCI</strong>,
    supports exactly one command queue, 32 commands deep — plenty for a mechanical
    drive that could barely keep that queue full, but a hard ceiling for flash.
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>NVMe</strong> (Non-Volatile Memory Express) throws out that legacy path
    entirely. It talks straight to the CPU over <strong>PCIe lanes</strong> instead
    of a storage-specific bus, and its command set was designed from scratch around
    what flash can actually do: up to <strong>65,535 queues</strong>, each up to
    <strong> 65,536 commands</strong> deep, so a modern multi-core CPU can issue
    requests to the drive in parallel instead of standing in one line.
  </p>

  <p>
    The gap isn't just bandwidth — it's overhead. Every AHCI command travels
    through a driver stack built for disks that took milliseconds to respond. NVMe
    strips that stack down to the operations flash actually needs, cutting CPU
    cycles spent per command and letting drives fully exploit PCIe's lanes as
    generations move from PCIe 3.0 to 4.0 and beyond.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="performance">
  <h2>Storage Performance: Latency and IOPS</h2>
  <p>
    Two numbers describe how "fast" a drive really feels in everyday use, and
    neither of them is the big MB/s figure on the box.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>Latency</strong> — the delay before a single request starts
      returning data. An HDD pays for a physical seek and rotational delay, often
      several milliseconds. Flash has no moving parts to wait for, so NVMe latency
      is typically measured in <strong>microseconds</strong>.
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>IOPS</strong> (Input/Output Operations Per Second) — how many
      individual read or write operations a drive can complete per second,
      especially with small, random requests. This is the metric that matters for
      databases, virtual machines, and boot times, where the workload is thousands
      of tiny scattered requests rather than one long stream.
    </li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    A 7,200 RPM HDD manages roughly 100–200 random IOPS, limited by how fast its
    arm can physically move. A SATA SSD reaches tens of thousands. An NVMe drive,
    freed from AHCI's single queue, can push past a million — not because flash
    reads any single cell faster than that, but because thousands of requests can
    be in flight across its parallel queues at once.
  </p>
</section>
