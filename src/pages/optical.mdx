<section id="intro">
  <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p>
    Magnetic storage was fast but fragile — disks warped, drives crashed, tapes degraded.
    In 1982, Sony and Philips answered with the <strong>Compact Disc</strong>: 650 MB on
    a coaster-sized disc, read by light alone, never touching the surface. It outlasted
    every magnetic medium of its era.
  </p>
  <p>
    Over 30 years, optical storage evolved through three generations — CD (1982),
    DVD (1995), and Blu-ray (2006) — each squeezing more data onto the same disc by
    using shorter and shorter wavelengths of laser light.
  </p>
</section>

  <p>
    Data is stamped into the polycarbonate as a spiral of microscopic bumps called
    <strong>pits</strong>, separated by flat areas called <strong>lands</strong>.
    A <strong>laser diode</strong> shines through the base onto the track — lands reflect
    the beam back cleanly (high signal), while pit edges scatter it (low signal).
    Those highs and lows become binary data.
  </p>

  <h3>Why Wavelength Matters</h3>
  <p>
    A laser can only resolve features about half its wavelength in size. Shorter = smaller
    pits = more data. CD uses <strong>780 nm</strong> (infrared), DVD uses <strong>650 nm</strong>
    (red), and Blu-ray uses <strong>405 nm</strong> (violet) — that's why capacity jumps
    so dramatically across formats.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="pits-lands">
  <h2>Pits and Lands as Binary Data</h2>
  <p>
    Common misconception: pits ≠ 1s and lands ≠ 0s. On real CDs, it's the
    <strong>transitions</strong> between pit and land that represent a
    <code style="color:var(--accent-green)">1</code> — staying on either surface
    (no transition) is a <code style="color:var(--accent-green)">0</code>.
    This is called <strong>NRZI</strong> encoding.
  </p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Noise immunity</strong> — no baseline needed between bits, easier to read at speed.</li>
    <li><strong>Self-clocking</strong> — the drive syncs its clock to the transitions themselves.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    Before hitting the disc, data also goes through <strong>Eight-to-Fourteen Modulation (EFM)</strong>,
    which converts each byte into a 14-bit pattern — guaranteeing no run of identical signal
    is too long or too short for the optics to track.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="standards">
  <h2>CD vs. DVD vs. Blu-ray</h2>
  <p>Shorter wavelength = smaller pits = more data. Same disc, totally different capacity:</p>

  <table class="compare-table">
    <thead>
      <tr>
        <th>Format</th>
        <th>Year</th>
        <th>Laser</th>
        <th>Wavelength</th>
        <th>Track pitch</th>
        <th>Min. pit size</th>
        <th>Capacity (1 layer)</th>
      </tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr>
        <td>CD</td>
        <td>1982</td>
        <td>Infrared</td>
        <td>780 nm</td>
        <td>1,600 nm</td>
        <td>833 nm</td>
        <td>700 MB</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>DVD</td>
        <td>1995</td>
        <td>Red</td>
        <td>650 nm</td>
        <td>740 nm</td>
        <td>400 nm</td>
        <td>4.7 GB</td>
      </tr>
      <tr>
        <td>Blu-ray</td>
        <td>2006</td>
        <td>Violet</td>
        <td>405 nm</td>
        <td>320 nm</td>
        <td>149 nm</td>
        <td>25 GB</td>
      </tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

  <p>
    The track pitch shrinks 5&times; from CD to Blu-ray, giving Blu-ray ~35&times; more
    capacity on the exact same diameter disc. The tradeoff: Blu-ray's thinner protective
    layer (0.1 mm vs. DVD's 0.6 mm) makes it more scratch-prone.
  </p>
</section>
