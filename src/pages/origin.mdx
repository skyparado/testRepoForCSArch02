<section id="intro">
  <h2>Introduction</h2>
  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    This section explores the earliest forms of computer data storage and processing.
    It examines how punch cards were used to encode and input data, and how magnetic
    drums served as one of the first electronic storage devices. These technologies
    established the foundations of data representation, storage, and retrieval in
    early computer systems.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="punch-cards">
  <h2>Punch Cards: Data Encoding and Processing</h2>
  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    Punch cards were one of the earliest methods used to encode and process digital information.
    Data and instructions were represented by holes punched into specific positions on a card,
    with each hole corresponding to a character or piece of information that machines
    could read automatically.
  </p>

  <ImageSlideshow client:load images={[punch.src, train.src]} alt="Punch card photo" />

  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    The idea was inspired by train conductors who punched holes
    into railway tickets to record passenger details. <strong>Herman Hollerith</strong> later adapted
    this concept to create machine-readable punch cards for the 1890 US Census, allowing
    data to be processed faster and more efficiently. IBM later standardized the technology
    through the <strong>IBM Computer Card</strong>, which became essential in early computer systems.
    Each card typically stored one line of program code or data, and stacks of cards
    were processed through card readers to input information into computers and tabulating machines.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="magnetic-drums">
  <h2>Magnetic Drums: Memory Operations</h2>
  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    The magnetic drum was invented by <strong>Gustav Tauschek in 1932</strong> and was later utilized by the <strong>US
    Navy during World War II</strong>. It served as an early form of computer memory and storage. The
    device consisted of a large metal cylinder coated with magnetic iron-oxide material, where
    information was stored through magnetic polarities.
  </p>

  <ImageSlideshow client:load images={[magneticDrums.src, drumsHistory.src]} alt="Magnetic drum photo" />

  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    Data was written on the outer surface of the
    drum, similar to how modern hard drives operate. However, unlike modern drives, the read/write
    heads remained fixed while the drum rotated rapidly. As the drum spun, the heads waited for the
    needed data to pass by in order to read or write information. Although magnetic drums could hold
    significantly more data than punch cards, their storage capacity was still very limited by today's
    standards, usually only a few kilobytes.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="batch-processing-systems">
  <h2>Batch Processing Systems</h2>
  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    Batch processing systems were early computing methods where multiple jobs were grouped together and processed
    automatically in sequence without direct user interaction. Punch cards were used to input programs and data,
    while magnetic drums stored the information during processing. Once loaded, the computer executed each job
    continuously in order, improving efficiency and reducing manual operation.
  </p>
</section>

<section id="sequential-data-access">
  <h2>Sequential Data Access</h2>
  <p style="text-align: justify;">
    Sequential data access was a method where data was stored and retrieved in a fixed order. Systems like punch
    cards and magnetic drums required earlier records to be read first before the desired information could be
    reached. This process made data access slower compared to modern random-access storage systems.
  </p>
</section>

  <p style="text-align: justify;">
